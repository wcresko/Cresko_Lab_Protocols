44  Adult Fish Anesthesia

Introduction

  • Purpose: This protocol describes how to safely anesthetize adult fish for imaging, measurements, surgical procedures, or other manipulations
  • Procedure Type: Husbandry/Anesthesia
  • Species:
    • Threespine stickleback (Gasterosteus aculeatus)
    • Bay pipefish (Syngnathus leptorhyncus)
    • Gulf pipefish (Syngnathus scovelli)
  • Author: Mark Currey
  • Date Created: February 28, 2024, updated November 22, 2025
  • IACUC Protocol: Required for all anesthesia procedures
Safety and Handling
  • Wear appropriate PPE (lab coat, gloves, safety glasses)
  • MS-222 (tricaine) can be absorbed through skin - always wear gloves
  • Work in well-ventilated area
  • Never consume food or drink in anesthesia work area
  • Dispose of MS-222 solutions according to chemical waste protocols
  • Keep MSDS (Material Safety Data Sheet) readily accessible
IACUC Compliance

All anesthesia procedures must be:

  • Covered by current IACUC protocol
  • Performed by trained personnel
  • Documented in laboratory records
  • Monitored for fish welfare
  • Ended with appropriate recovery or euthanasia

Anesthesia is not euthanasia: Fish must either fully recover or be properly euthanized. Prolonged anesthesia without recovery or euthanasia is not acceptable.

Background

MS-222 (tricaine methanesulfonate, also called tricaine or ethyl 3-aminobenzoate methanesulfonate) is the most commonly used anesthetic for fish. It is FDA-approved for use in fish and acts as a central nervous system depressant, inducing a dose-dependent sedation and anesthesia.

Mechanism of Action

MS-222 works by blocking sodium channels in nerve cell membranes, similar to local anesthetics like lidocaine. At appropriate concentrations, it produces:

  • Light sedation (reduced activity, maintained equilibrium)
  • Deep sedation (loss of equilibrium, reduced opercular rate)
  • Surgical anesthesia (no response to stimuli, slow opercular rate)
  • Overdose/death (cessation of opercular movements)

The goal for most procedures is to achieve deep sedation or light surgical anesthesia - enough to immobilize the fish for manipulation but maintaining respiratory movements.

Factors Affecting Anesthesia

  • Water temperature: Warmer water increases metabolic rate and speeds induction
  • Fish size: Smaller fish require less time to anesthetize
  • Fish health: Stressed or sick fish are more sensitive
  • Water chemistry: pH, salinity, oxygen levels all affect uptake
  • Individual variation: Always monitor each fish individually

Materials

Equipment

  • Small containers or petri dishes (for anesthesia)
  • Larger containers or tanks (for recovery)
  • Transfer pipettes or small nets
  • Timer or clock
  • Thermometer
  • pH meter or pH strips
  • Imaging equipment or other experimental apparatus (as needed)

Optional

  • Dissecting microscope (for detailed observation)
  • Small sponge or soft surface (for positioning during imaging)
  • Gloves (nitrile or latex)

Solutions

MS-222 Stock Solution (4g/L)

Pharmaceutical Grade Tricaine

CRITICAL: Tricaine must be pharmaceutical-grade. We use tricaine purchased from Pentair (manufactured by Western Chemical, FDA approved). Tricaine (3-amino benzoic acid ethyl ester, also called ethyl m-aminobenzoate) comes in powdered form. Purchase the smallest amount possible because tricaine expires quickly (typically 1-2 years from manufacture date).

  • Check expiration date before use
  • Store powder at 4°C in original container
  • Protect from light and moisture
  • Discard if discolored or clumped

Tris-Buffered MS-222 Stock (4g/L)

-   4 g tricaine powder
-   979 ml deionized (DD) water
-   ~21 ml 1 M Tris (pH 9)
-   Adjust pH to ~7.0
-   Aliquot in 50ml tubes
-   Label with "MS-222 Stock Solution 4g/L" and date
-   Store at -20°C
-   This makes 1 liter of stock solution
-   Stable for 6 months when frozen
Why Buffer MS-222?

Unbuffered MS-222 is very acidic (pH ~3), which is stressful to fish and less effective as an anesthetic. Buffering to neutral pH (7.0-7.5) with Tris improves efficacy and reduces stress. Always check pH before use.

Anesthesia Working Solution

The standard anesthesia concentration for adult fish is 168 mg/L (also written as 0.168 g/L or 168 ppm).

  • Stickleback
  • Pipefish/Syngnathid

For 100 ml working solution:

  • 92.5 ml of 3 ppt Instant Ocean solution
  • 7.5 ml of MS-222 stock (4g/L)
  • Final concentration: 168 mg/L in 3 ppt salinity

Prepare 3 ppt Instant Ocean:

  • 0.3 g Instant Ocean salt mix
  • 100 ml deionized water
  • Mix until fully dissolved

Storage:

  • Store in amber (dark) bottle to protect from light
  • Label with: “MS-222 Anesthesia 168 mg/L”, preparation date, expiration date (6 months)
  • Keep at room temperature or 4°C
  • Discard if discolored, precipitated, or past expiration

For 100 ml working solution:

  • 92.5 ml of 25-30 ppt Instant Ocean solution
  • 7.5 ml of MS-222 stock (4g/L)
  • Final concentration: 168 mg/L in 25-30 ppt salinity

Prepare 25-30 ppt Instant Ocean:

  • 2.5-3.0 g Instant Ocean salt mix
  • 100 ml deionized water
  • Mix until fully dissolved

Notes for Syngnathids:

  • Use full-strength seawater (25-30 ppt) to match their osmotic requirements
  • Bay pipefish are more sensitive - may need lower concentration (start with 120-140 mg/L)
  • Gulf pipefish tolerate wider salinity range but prefer 25-30 ppt

Storage:

  • Store in amber bottle to protect from light
  • Label with: “MS-222 Anesthesia 168 mg/L”, preparation date, expiration date (6 months)
  • Keep at room temperature or 4°C

Recovery Water

Prepare fresh water appropriate for your species:

  • Stickleback: 3 ppt Instant Ocean or system water
  • Pipefish: 25-30 ppt Instant Ocean or system water
  • Use same temperature as holding system
  • Ensure adequate oxygenation (aeration or frequent water changes)

Procedure

Pre-Anesthesia Preparation

Planning Ahead
  • Know exactly what procedure you will perform and estimate duration
  • Have all equipment ready before anesthetizing fish
  • Work quickly but carefully - minimize anesthesia time
  • Target total anesthesia time: <10 minutes for routine procedures
  • Plan recovery container and monitoring schedule

  1. Prepare workspace

    • Set up anesthesia container (petri dish or small container)
    • Set up recovery container with fresh, aerated water
    • Prepare imaging or experimental equipment
    • Ensure adequate lighting
    • Have timer ready

  2. Prepare solutions

    • Pour ~50-100 ml MS-222 anesthesia solution into anesthesia container
    • Fill recovery container with fresh water (200-500 ml, depending on fish size)
    • Verify temperature is appropriate (16-20°C for stickleback, 18-22°C for pipefish)
    • Optional: Check pH of anesthesia solution (should be 7.0-7.5)

  3. Select and observe fish

    • Choose healthy fish only - do not anesthetize sick or stressed fish
    • Observe for 5 minutes: normal swimming, feeding, coloration
    • Record fish ID or description
    • Note start time

Anesthesia Induction

  1. Transfer fish to anesthesia solution

    • Use transfer pipette or small net
    • Minimize air exposure
    • Gently place fish in anesthesia container
    • Start timer immediately

  2. Monitor anesthesia stages

    Stages of Anesthesia

    Stage 1: Light Sedation (0-2 minutes)

    • Decreased swimming activity
    • Decreased response to external stimuli
    • Normal opercular rate
    • Normal equilibrium

    Stage 2: Deep Sedation (2-4 minutes)

    • Increased loss of muscle tone
    • Erratic swimming
    • Decreased opercular rate
    • Loss of equilibrium (fish tips on side or inverts)

    Stage 3: Surgical Anesthesia (4-6 minutes)

    • Total loss of muscle tone
    • No response to handling
    • Slow, regular opercular movements
    • No reflex response to tail pinch

    Stage 4: Overdose

    • Opercular movements very slow or stopped
    • Cardiac arrest (if prolonged)
    • AVOID THIS STAGE unless euthanizing

  3. Achieve appropriate anesthesia depth

    • For imaging or measurements: Stage 2 (loss of equilibrium) is usually sufficient
    • For surgery or manipulations: Stage 3 (surgical anesthesia) required
    • Key indicator: Fish should not respond to gentle handling but maintain slow opercular movements
    • Typical induction time: 3-6 minutes for adults (faster for smaller fish)

During Anesthesia

  1. Perform procedure

    • Work quickly but carefully
    • Keep fish moist if out of water
      • Use transfer pipettes to drip anesthesia solution on gills
      • Can place fish on damp sponge or soft surface
      • Never let fish dry out
    • Monitor opercular movements continuously
    • Target procedure time: <5 minutes for routine work, <10 minutes maximum
Monitoring During Anesthesia

CRITICAL MONITORING:

  • Opercular (gill cover) movements must continue throughout procedure
  • If opercular movements stop: immediately transfer to recovery water
  • If opercular movements become very slow (<10 per minute): prepare to end procedure
  • If fish appears too deeply anesthetized: transfer to recovery water immediately

Normal opercular rates during anesthesia:

  • Light sedation: 50-80% of normal rate
  • Deep sedation: 30-50% of normal rate
  • Surgical anesthesia: 20-40% of normal rate
  • Danger zone: <20% of normal rate

  1. Complete procedure

    • Finish procedure as quickly as possible
    • Record any observations or measurements
    • Take photos if needed
    • Note total anesthesia time

Recovery

  1. Transfer to recovery water

    • Gently transfer fish using pipette or soft net
    • Place in recovery container with fresh, aerated water
    • Position fish upright if needed
    • Note time of transfer to recovery

  2. Support recovery

    • If fish is deeply anesthetized, gently move through water to pass water over gills
    • Hold fish upright with head facing into gentle current (if using airstone)
    • Can use transfer pipette to gently direct water flow over gills
    • Continue until fish shows signs of increased opercular rate

  3. Monitor recovery progression

    Stages of Recovery

    Early Recovery (0-5 minutes):

    • Opercular rate increases
    • May remain on side or inverted
    • No swimming movements

    Mid Recovery (5-10 minutes):

    • Opercular rate near normal
    • Fish rights itself
    • Weak swimming movements begin

    Late Recovery (10-20 minutes):

    • Normal opercular rate
    • Active swimming
    • Maintains equilibrium
    • May still have reduced response to external stimuli

    Full Recovery (20+ minutes):

    • Normal swimming behavior
    • Normal response to stimuli
    • Normal coloration

  4. Check recovery every 2-5 minutes

    • Look for increasing opercular rate
    • Watch for return of swimming ability
    • Note return of equilibrium
    • Check for normal coloration
    • Typical recovery time: 5-20 minutes
Recovery Problems

If fish is not recovering:

  • Ensure water is well-oxygenated (add airstone if not present)
  • Check water temperature (should match system temperature)
  • Gently move fish through water to increase gill perfusion
  • Continue support for up to 30 minutes
  • If no signs of recovery after 30 minutes, consult with supervisor

Signs of successful recovery:

  • Normal opercular rate
  • Active swimming
  • Attempts to evade handling
  • Normal coloration (not pale or dark)

Post-Recovery

  1. Return to system or continue monitoring

    • Once fully recovered (normal swimming, equilibrium, responsiveness), fish can be returned to home tank
    • Alternative: Keep in recovery tank for additional monitoring (2-24 hours)
    • Monitor during next daily health check
    • Record successful recovery and any observations

  2. Documentation

    • Record in laboratory notebook or database:
      • Fish ID
      • Date and time
      • Anesthesia concentration and duration
      • Procedure performed
      • Recovery time
      • Any complications or observations
    • Update IACUC records if required

Concentration Adjustments

When to Use Lower Concentrations

  • Very small fish (<30 mm)
  • Fish in poor health or stressed
  • Prolonged procedures (>10 minutes)
  • Species known to be sensitive (e.g., bay pipefish)

Suggested lower dose: 120-140 mg/L

When to Use Higher Concentrations

  • Very large fish (>60 mm)
  • Fish resistant to anesthesia
  • Quick procedures (<2 minutes)

Suggested higher dose: 200-250 mg/L

Warning

Always start with standard concentration (168 mg/L) for new species or procedures. Adjust only after observing response. Never exceed 300 mg/L for routine anesthesia.

Special Considerations

  • Stickleback
  • Pipefish/Syngnathid

Anesthesia Response: - Generally robust and tolerate anesthesia well - Adult males in breeding condition may be more sensitive - Spines can make handling difficult - use soft nets or careful hand positioning

Recovery: - Usually quick and uneventful - Can be quite active immediately upon recovery - use covered containers to prevent jumping

Typical Timings: - Induction: 3-5 minutes - Recovery: 5-15 minutes

Anesthesia Response: - More delicate than stickleback - require careful monitoring - Bay pipefish (S. leptorhyncus) may be more sensitive than gulf pipefish - Body shape makes it difficult to assess equilibrium loss - watch for cessation of swimming

Recovery: - May be slower than stickleback (10-20 minutes) - Often settle to bottom during recovery - this is normal - Pregnant males: extra care to avoid embryo loss

Typical Timings: - Induction: 4-6 minutes (bay pipefish often faster) - Recovery: 10-20 minutes

Special Notes: - Use full-strength seawater (25-30 ppt) - Ensure water temperature matches system (18-22°C) - Can be more prone to jumping during light sedation - use covered containers

Alternative Anesthetics

While MS-222 is the standard, other anesthetics are occasionally used:

Clove Oil (Eugenol)

  • Natural anesthetic derived from clove plants
  • Less expensive than MS-222
  • Must be dissolved in ethanol first (1:10 clove oil:ethanol)
  • Working concentration: 40-100 mg/L
  • Not FDA approved for fish
  • Recovery can be longer than MS-222

2-Phenoxyethanol

  • Alternative to MS-222
  • Working concentration: 0.1-0.5 ml/L
  • Less commonly used
  • Not FDA approved
Note

This protocol focuses on MS-222 as it is FDA-approved and most commonly used in research settings. Consult with supervisor and IACUC before using alternative anesthetics.

Quality Control

  • Test anesthesia solution with control fish before critical experiments
  • Keep log of induction and recovery times to track consistency
  • Make fresh working solutions every 6 months
  • Check pH of stock solutions periodically
  • Maintain accurate records for IACUC compliance
